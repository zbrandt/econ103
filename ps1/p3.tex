\begin{homeworkProblem}
    Let \(f : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}\) be a twice-differentiable function. 
    Show that \(\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x \partial y} f \geq 0\) if and only 
    if for every \(x, x', y, y' \in \mathbb{R}\):

    \[
        x' > x \text{ and } y' > y \Rightarrow f(x', y') - f(x, y') \geq 
        f(x', y) - f(x, y).
    \]

    \solution

    \begin{proof}
        To prove the if and only if condition, both directions need to be 
        proven. 

        \begin{itemize}
            \item[$\Rightarrow$] \textbf{Forward direction:} Assume that 
                $\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x \partial y} f \geq 0$ everywhere.
                By the Fundamental Theoreom of Calculus, 

                \[
                    f'(x', y) - f'(x, y) = \int_{x}^{x'} \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x \partial y}(t, y) \, dt
                \]

                Since $\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x \partial y} \geq 0$, 
                $f'(x', y) - f'(x, y) \geq 0$, i.e., $f(x', y) - f(x, y)$ is 
                non-decreasing in $y$. And since $y' > y$, this forward
                direction is proven to hold.  

                \[
                    f(x', y') - f(x, y') \geq f(x', y) - f(x, y))
                \]
            
            \item[$\Leftarrow$] \textbf{Reverse direction:} Now assume that the 
                inequality holds for all $x' > x$ and $y' > y$. Define $x'$ as 
                $x_0 + h$ and $y'$ as $y_0 + k$ where $h, k > 0$. The 
                inequality can be rearranged to find partial derivatives in
                terms of $y$ as $k \to 0^+$. 

                
                \[
                    \begin{split}
                        f(x_0 + h, y_0 + k) - f(x_0, y_0 + k) &\geq f(x_0 + h, y_0) - f(x_0, y_0) \\
                        \frac{f(x_0 + h, y_0 + k) - f(x_0 + h, y_0)}{k} &\geq \frac{f(x_0, y_0 + k) - f(x_0, y_0)}{k} \\
                        \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}(x_0 + h, y_0) &\geq \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}(x_0, y_0)
                    \end{split}
                \]
                
                This shows that $\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}$ is 
                non-decreasing in $x$ for fixed $y$. Therefore:

                \[
                    \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\right) = 
                    \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y \partial x} \geq 0
                \]

        \end{itemize}

        So both directions have been shown to hold and the claim has been
        proven. 
    \end{proof}

\end{homeworkProblem}
